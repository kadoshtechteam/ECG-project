INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the Study
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) continue to be the leading cause of mortality globally, accounting for approximately 17.9 million deaths annually, as reported by the World Health Organization (2025). Among these, heart attacks—also known as myocardial infarctions—represent one of the most critical and life-threatening manifestations. A substantial number of these deaths occur outside hospital settings due to delayed detection and lack of immediate intervention (Ojha and Dhamoon, 2023). The ability to monitor cardiac function continuously and detect early signs of potential heart failure or arrhythmia is, therefore, not just a medical necessity but a public health priority.
Traditionally, electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring is conducted in clinical environments using stationary equipment that requires professional interpretation. While these systems are effective in diagnosing cardiovascular anomalies, they are largely inaccessible to populations in remote or under-resourced regions. Moreover, conventional ECG systems are limited to point-in-time diagnostics rather than continuous, real-time monitoring, thereby increasing the risk of undetected arrhythmic events or sudden cardiac arrests (Serhani et al., 2020).
In recent years, the convergence of Internet of Things (IoT) technology and machine learning (ML) has revolutionized the landscape of digital healthcare. IoT allows for the development of compact, wearable, and cost-effective health monitoring devices capable of transmitting physiological data in real time (Aslam and Jack, 2025). These devices empower users by offering continuous health surveillance, remote diagnostics, and early warning systems, often integrated into mobile applications for enhanced usability. Concurrently, machine learning—particularly deep learning techniques—has emerged as a powerful tool for analyzing complex biomedical signals such as ECG waveforms. Deep learning models like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are especially effective in capturing spatial and temporal dependencies within ECG data, making them ideal for automated anomaly detection and heart condition classification (Wu and Guo, 2025).
This project harnesses these technological advancements by proposing an IoT-based Heart Monitoring and Heart-Attack Detection System that combines ECG signal acquisition with an intelligent classification model based on CNN-LSTM architecture. The system is designed to be portable, affordable, and user-friendly, targeting both medically trained users and laypersons. At its core, the solution employs an AD8232 ECG sensor connected to a NodeMCU ESP8266 microcontroller, which acquires the heart's electrical signals and transmits them wirelessly to a mobile device. A mobile application, built using React Native, performs real-time visualization and integrates a pre-trained CNN-LSTM model using TensorFlow Lite for on-device inference. This allows for offline, low-latency risk analysis without reliance on continuous internet connectivity.
The importance of such a system is underscored by its potential to detect early signs of arrhythmia, tachycardia, bradycardia, and other ECG abnormalities in real time, thus reducing the diagnostic burden on healthcare systems and enabling timely medical intervention. It not only bridges the accessibility gap for cardiac diagnostics but also contributes to the broader goal of preventive and personalized medicine.

A portable, IoT-based heart monitoring and detection system that integrates real-time ECG data acquisition with predictive analysis using deep learning can address this gap. LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Cardiovascular Diseases
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) encompass a wide spectrum of disorders that affect the heart and blood vessels, including coronary artery disease, hypertension, cardiomyopathies, and arrhythmias. Globally, CVDs remain the leading cause of death, with approximately 18 million deaths recorded annually (World Health Organization, 2025). In Nigeria, the rising prevalence of CVDs has been attributed to factors such as urbanization, sedentary lifestyles, inadequate access to early diagnostics, and limited public awareness (Amadi et al., 2022).
Among the most critical manifestations of CVDs is myocardial infarction, or heart attack, which occurs when blood flow to a section of the heart is obstructed, typically due to plaque buildup in the coronary arteries (Ojha and Dhamoon, 2023). While heart attacks are widely recognized, disturbances in the heart’s electrical rhythm—known collectively as arrhythmias—are equally significant and can result in serious health consequences if undetected or untreated.
Tachycardia is defined as a resting heart rate exceeding 100 beats per minute. Though it can be a normal physiological response to stress or exercise, persistent sinus tachycardia may indicate underlying conditions such as heart failure, anemia, or thyroid disorders (Henning and Krawiec, 2023). On the other end of the spectrum is bradycardia, which refers to a slower-than-normal heart rate, typically below 60 beats per minute. While bradycardia is common in well-trained athletes, it can also signify conduction system diseases or adverse drug effects. In underserved regions like Nigeria, the diagnosis and treatment of bradycardia are hindered by the high cost and unavailability of pacemaker therapy, leaving many patients untreated (Egas et al., 2023).
Arrhythmia is a broad term for irregular heart rhythms—either too fast, too slow, or erratic in pattern. These abnormalities can impair the heart's ability to pump blood efficiently and may present as palpitations, dizziness, syncope, or sudden cardiac arrest. Although some arrhythmias are benign, others may be life-threatening, underscoring the need for accurate and early detection. Modern classification techniques, including artificial intelligence models, have significantly improved the ability to detect and categorize arrhythmic patterns in ECG signals (Saber and Abotaleb, 2022).
Atrial fibrillation (AFib), the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, is particularly concerning due to its association with stroke, heart failure, and increased mortality. It is characterized by rapid, disorganized electrical activity in the atria, leading to ineffective atrial contractions. On ECG, AFib is identified by the absence of distinct P waves and irregular R-R intervals. The condition may be asymptomatic or present with fatigue, palpitations, and shortness of breath. Despite its clinical significance, AFib remains underdiagnosed in many low-resource settings due to limited screening and monitoring tools (Pellman and Sheikh, 2015).
Given the complexity and often silent nature of these heart rhythm abnormalities, continuous monitoring and early detection are critical. However, in Nigeria and similar low-income countries, access to such technologies is severely limited. The integration of intelligent ECG monitoring systems into routine healthcare has the potential to revolutionize cardiac care by enabling timely intervention, reducing mortality, and bridging the diagnostic gap in resource-constrained environments.

2.1.1 Common Causes of Cardiovascular Diseases
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) result from a complex interplay of genetic, behavioral, metabolic, and environmental risk factors. Understanding these causes is critical to designing effective prevention and intervention strategies, especially in developing countries like Nigeria, where the burden of CVDs is escalating rapidly. Hypertension is one of the most common and dangerous contributors to CVDs. Often asymptomatic in its early stages, hypertension damages the heart and blood vessels over time, increasing the risk of stroke, heart failure, and myocardial infarction. Community-based screening studies have shown that many individuals remain unaware of their elevated blood pressure until complications arise, making public education and early detection essential (Ernawati et al., 2023).
Lifestyle-related factors—including physical inactivity, poor diet, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption—are strongly linked to cardiovascular risk. These behaviors contribute to the development of obesity, metabolic syndrome, and vascular dysfunction, all of which are precursors to heart disease. Sedentary behavior and unhealthy eating patterns have been particularly implicated in the rise of CVD incidence globally (Jafari et al., 2023). Diabetes mellitus also plays a major role in the onset and progression of cardiovascular complications. Chronically elevated blood glucose levels damage endothelial linings, promote plaque formation in arteries, and accelerate the development of atherosclerosis. Diabetic patients are significantly more likely to experience myocardial infarction or heart failure compared to non-diabetic individuals (Matheus et al., 2013).
Another critical factor is dyslipidemia, characterized by elevated levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and reduced high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol. This imbalance contributes to the buildup of arterial plaque and narrows blood vessels, disrupting normal circulation and increasing the likelihood of ischemic events such as stroke and heart attack (Pappan et al., 2025). Chronic stress has also emerged as a powerful, albeit less visible, driver of cardiovascular disease. Psychosocial stress triggers the release of cortisol and activates sympathetic nervous system responses, which elevate blood pressure, induce inflammation, and impair vascular function. Over time, this stress burden can translate into tangible cardiovascular damage (Dar et al., 2019).
Additionally, lack of awareness and limited access to health education continue to hinder prevention efforts in many communities. Studies show that a significant portion of the population—particularly women and young adults—remain uninformed about the symptoms and risk factors associated with cardiovascular disease (Alshakarah et al., 2023). This knowledge gap contributes to late diagnoses and poor outcomes, especially in regions with fragile healthcare infrastructure. 

2.2 Role of IoT in Healthcare
The Internet of Things (IoT) has emerged as one of the most transformative innovations in modern healthcare, offering the potential to significantly improve patient outcomes, reduce healthcare costs, and expand access to medical services—particularly in under-resourced environments. IoT in healthcare refers to a system of interconnected medical devices, sensors, communication technologies, and cloud-based platforms that enable real-time health monitoring, diagnostics, and data sharing (Abdulmalek et al., 2022).
One of the most notable applications of IoT is in remote patient monitoring (RPM), where wearable devices collect physiological signals such as heart rate, ECG, blood glucose, and oxygen saturation, and wirelessly transmit them to healthcare providers. This facilitates real-time health tracking without requiring patients to be physically present at a medical facility, a feature that is especially beneficial for managing chronic conditions and serving patients in rural or remote areas (Kanade and Kumar, 2025).
IoT also plays a crucial role in enhancing personalized and preventive care. For example, smart ECG devices connected via Bluetooth or Wi-Fi can identify abnormal cardiac rhythms and notify users or clinicians instantly. This supports early intervention and empowers patients to manage their health proactively. The integration of AI-powered wearable sensors further enhances the accuracy and intelligence of these devices by allowing them to adaptively learn and detect clinically significant patterns from physiological data (Shajari et al., 2023).
In addition, IoT enables real-time clinical decision-making by providing continuous streams of patient data that can be analyzed using artificial intelligence and machine learning. These analytics can reveal trends in patient behavior, predict adverse health events, and offer insights into treatment effectiveness and population health outcomes (Nalluri, 2024). For example, AI algorithms can process ECG data collected by IoT sensors to detect arrhythmias or early signs of myocardial infarction with high precision, facilitating timely medical intervention and reducing the risk of complications.
IoT is also revolutionizing emergency response systems. Devices equipped with GPS modules and real-time communication capabilities can transmit distress alerts and vital data to emergency medical teams when a patient's condition deteriorates. This is particularly crucial for high-risk individuals who may experience sudden cardiac events. Integrating IoT into emergency infrastructure ensures that responders have timely and accurate patient data even before arriving on the scene, enabling faster and more effective care (Iseal and Joel, 2024).
From a hospital management standpoint, IoT enhances operational efficiency by supporting asset tracking, predictive equipment maintenance, and smart scheduling of medical staff. These functions help reduce administrative burden and improve service delivery in healthcare systems.
Despite its transformative potential, the adoption of IoT in healthcare does face challenges, particularly regarding data privacy, interoperability, and infrastructure limitations. In many developing nations, including Nigeria, consistent power supply and internet connectivity can be unreliable, which impacts the functionality of cloud-based systems. However, recent advancements in edge computing and offline-capable machine learning models—such as those powered by TensorFlow Lite—are helping to bridge this gap by enabling localized data processing and decision-making (Abdulmalek et al., 2022). In cardiovascular care, IoT-based systems combining ECG monitoring with intelligent mobile applications are proving to be cost-effective, accurate, and scalable. Such solutions are particularly valuable in addressing the growing burden of heart disease in underserved communities by bringing diagnostics and preventive care directly into the hands of patients.

2.3 Electrocardiogram (ECG) Signal Characteristics
An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a vital diagnostic tool used to record the electrical activity of the heart over time. By measuring voltage changes on the skin resulting from cardiac depolarization and repolarization, ECGs provide critical insight into the rhythm, rate, and overall function of the heart. These signals are typically captured through surface electrodes placed at specific locations on the body, and the resulting waveforms reflect different phases of the cardiac cycle (Sattar and Chhabra, 2025). Interpreting these waveforms allows clinicians to detect arrhythmias, conduction abnormalities, myocardial infarction, electrolyte imbalances, and structural heart changes. A clear understanding of ECG signal characteristics—including waveform morphology, interval durations, and spatial relationships—is fundamental for accurate cardiac monitoring and diagnosis, particularly in systems designed for automated anomaly detection.
2.3.1 Basic Components of the ECG Waveform
A typical ECG trace consists of a repeating sequence of distinct waveform elements that correspond to specific physiological events during the cardiac cycle. These waveforms reflect the electrical conduction through different chambers of the heart, offering a non-invasive yet highly informative view into cardiac health. Clinically, each component of the ECG signal carries diagnostic relevance for identifying rhythm disturbances, conduction delays, ischemic changes, and other pathological conditions (Sattar and Chhabra, 2025).
Recent advances in biomedical engineering and machine learning have made it possible to automate ECG interpretation by analyzing waveform morphology. Techniques such as support vector machines (SVM) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) rely heavily on accurate detection and segmentation of these wave components to classify normal and abnormal cardiac rhythms (Li et al., 2022).
 
Figure 2.1 Schematic diagram of normal sinus rhythm for a human heart as seen on the electrocardiogram (ECG) (Saab et al., 2016)
The fundamental elements of the ECG waveform include:
1.	P wave: This represents atrial depolarization, the electrical activation of the atria. It is usually the first positive deflection in the ECG waveform and indicates that the sinoatrial (SA) node has initiated the heartbeat. The shape and duration of the P wave can reveal atrial enlargement or conduction delays.
2.	QRS complex: This sharp, rapid deflection corresponds to ventricular depolarization. It is typically the most prominent portion of the ECG and includes three parts: a downward Q wave, an upward R wave, and a downward S wave. The QRS complex indicates how efficiently the electrical impulse spreads through the ventricles. Abnormal duration or morphology may suggest bundle branch blocks, ventricular hypertrophy, or myocardial infarction.
3.	T wave: The T wave reflects ventricular repolarization, or the resetting of the ventricular muscle fibers in preparation for the next contraction. It is typically a broad, upward deflection. Inverted or flattened T waves may indicate ischemia, electrolyte imbalance, or other pathology.
4.	U wave (if present): A small, often overlooked wave that sometimes follows the T wave, the U wave may represent delayed repolarization of the Purkinje fibers. It is not always visible and can vary based on heart rate and electrolyte levels.
5.	PR interval: This interval spans from the beginning of the P wave to the start of the QRS complex. It represents the time taken for the electrical impulse to travel from the atria to the ventricles via the atrioventricular (AV) node. A prolonged PR interval may suggest first-degree heart block.
6.	ST segment: Found between the end of the S wave and the start of the T wave, the ST segment represents the period when the ventricles are depolarized and contracting. ST segment elevation or depression is a critical marker in diagnosing acute myocardial infarction or ischemia.
7.	QT interval: This interval extends from the start of the QRS complex to the end of the T wave, encompassing both ventricular depolarization and repolarization. Abnormally prolonged QT intervals may increase the risk of arrhythmias such as torsades de pointes.

2.3.2 Signal Acquisition and Lead Configuration
Accurate electrocardiogram (ECG) analysis begins with proper signal acquisition, which involves the use of electrodes to detect the heart’s electrical activity and convert it into readable waveforms. These signals are typically measured as voltage differences between pairs of electrodes placed at strategic points on the body. The quality of the acquired ECG signal is critical for diagnostic reliability, especially in automated systems that rely on precise waveform morphology for classification.
ECG signal acquisition relies on biopotential electrodes—usually surface electrodes—that pick up the minute electrical impulses generated during cardiac depolarization and repolarization. These impulses are typically in the range of 0.5 to 5 millivolts and require amplification and filtering to become interpretable. The analog front-end circuitry, such as the AD8232 module used in this project, is specifically designed to handle low-amplitude cardiac signals while filtering out noise and motion artifacts. Shielding and driven-right-leg (DRL) circuitry are also important in minimizing common-mode noise and improving signal quality during acquisition (Jiang et al., 2018).
Electrode configuration plays a key role in defining the number and type of leads generated. The lead system refers to the spatial orientation from which the electrical activity of the heart is viewed. The most basic setup is the 3-lead configuration, which typically includes electrodes placed on the right arm (RA), left arm (LA), and left leg (LL). These leads form the basis for the standard limb leads: Lead I (LA–RA), Lead II (LL–RA), and Lead III (LL–LA). More advanced clinical settings employ 5-lead or 12-lead systems to provide additional perspectives of cardiac activity. Studies show that increasing the number and placement of electrodes significantly improves diagnostic fidelity and supports more complete reconstruction of heart vector data (Butchy et al., 2023).
The 3-lead system, however, offers a sufficient and cost-effective approach for basic rhythm monitoring and is widely used in wearable or ambulatory ECG devices. The minimal setup allows for lightweight design and reduced power consumption, which are important factors in IoT-based health monitoring systems. Nevertheless, one limitation of reduced-lead systems is that they provide a limited view of the heart’s electrical activity, potentially missing localized anomalies detectable only in precordial leads (chest leads).
Signal quality in portable systems can be affected by several factors, including motion artifacts, muscle noise (EMG interference), power line interference (50/60 Hz), and baseline wander due to respiration. These sources of noise can severely degrade signal integrity and lead to false or missed detections in automated analysis systems. Advanced filtering techniques, such as adaptive noise cancellation and moving average filters, are often used to mitigate these effects (Boyer et al., 2023). In real-time systems, signal acquisition must strike a balance between high sampling rates—typically in the range of 250 to 500 Hz—and low power consumption. In this project, a sampling frequency of 250 Hz was selected as a suitable compromise, ensuring sufficient temporal resolution for accurate QRS complex detection without overloading the microcontroller’s memory and processing capacity. 

2.3.3 Einthoven’s Triangle and ECG Lead Interpretation
A fundamental concept in ECG interpretation is Einthoven’s Triangle, a geometrical and physiological model introduced by Dutch physiologist Willem Einthoven, who was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1924 for his pioneering work in electrocardiography (Stec and Mazurak, 2024). This triangle is formed by placing electrodes on the right arm (RA), left arm (LA), and left leg (LL), generating three standard limb leads: Lead I (LA–RA), Lead II (LL–RA), and Lead III (LL–LA). These leads form the sides of an equilateral triangle surrounding the heart, providing different directional views of the electrical activity across the frontal plane.
Einthoven’s Triangle is the basis of the bipolar limb lead system, which allows clinicians to interpret the magnitude and direction of cardiac electrical impulses from three perspectives. Lead I measures electrical activity from the right to left arm, Lead II from the right arm to left leg, and Lead III from the left arm to left leg. Among these, Lead II is often preferred for rhythm monitoring due to its alignment with the natural direction of cardiac depolarization from the sinoatrial node through the ventricles (Jin et al., 2012).
 
Figure 2.2 Einthoven’s Triangle
This model simplifies the understanding of ECG signal directionality and facilitates vector-based interpretation of the heart’s electrical conduction. Clinicians analyze the relative amplitudes and polarities of the QRS complex across the three leads to assess axis deviation, chamber enlargement, and the presence of conditions such as bundle branch blocks or myocardial infarction. In rhythm analysis, comparing signals across multiple leads enhances the ability to distinguish between supraventricular and ventricular arrhythmias.
While Einthoven’s Triangle assumes a single, stationary dipole representing the heart's electrical activity, modern research has examined the limitations of this hypothesis. Critics argue that the human body introduces asymmetries and complexities not accounted for in the original model. Nonetheless, the triangular framework remains an effective simplification for practical use, especially in portable monitoring devices and IoT-based applications (Gargiulo et al., 2018).
In systems like the one developed in this project, which utilizes a 3-lead ECG configuration, the principles of Einthoven’s Triangle are directly applied. This approach ensures that minimal hardware can still yield clinically meaningful information about heart rhythm and conduction. By interpreting electrical signals through the triangular model, the device can track the direction and strength of cardiac vectors in real-time, making it highly suitable for ambulatory and low-resource healthcare environments.

2.4 Review of Existing Heart Monitoring Technologies
The evolution of heart monitoring technologies has significantly improved the diagnosis and management of cardiovascular diseases over the last several decades. From bulky analog machines to lightweight wearable devices, the development trajectory reflects an increasing demand for real-time, remote, and patient-centered care. Traditional hospital-based systems still serve as the clinical gold standard for comprehensive cardiac assessment, but the rise of mobile health (mHealth) solutions, wearable biosensors, and AI-powered monitoring has enabled more accessible and scalable alternatives. 
2.4.1 Traditional Hospital-Based Monitoring Systems
Traditional hospital-based heart monitoring systems are designed for high-accuracy diagnostics and remain the benchmark for clinical cardiac assessment. The most commonly used system is the 12-lead electrocardiograph, which captures the heart’s electrical activity from multiple angles using electrodes placed on the limbs and chest. This configuration enables detailed spatial analysis of electrical conduction and is essential for diagnosing complex conditions such as myocardial infarction, bundle branch blocks, ventricular hypertrophy, and atrial fibrillation (Serhani et al., 2020). The comprehensive coverage provided by the 12-lead system allows clinicians to localize ischemic regions and assess axis deviations with high precision.
Another staple of clinical cardiology is the Holter monitor, a portable device that records continuous ECG activity over a 24 to 48-hour period. Holter monitors are particularly valuable for patients experiencing intermittent or episodic symptoms such as palpitations, syncope, or dizziness, which may not be detectable during a standard ECG conducted in the clinic. Patients wear the device while performing everyday activities, and the data is later analyzed to correlate symptoms with cardiac rhythm abnormalities (Mubarik and Iqbal, 2025). Similarly, event recorders—which may be worn for weeks—are manually triggered by patients when symptoms occur, capturing only brief segments of ECG data during those moments.
Despite their diagnostic accuracy and clinical relevance, these hospital-based and ambulatory systems present several limitations. First, they require skilled healthcare professionals for electrode placement, data interpretation, and system maintenance, creating operational challenges in rural or resource-constrained environments (Serhani et al., 2020). Second, the devices are typically expensive, non-interactive, and often lack real-time data transmission or automatic alerting capabilities. Most Holter and event monitors also require patients to return to healthcare facilities for data retrieval and analysis, which can delay the diagnosis and treatment of critical cardiac events. Moreover, the physical design and size of many traditional ECG systems make them less suitable for continuous or long-term monitoring, especially outside the clinical setting. In developing countries, these challenges are further exacerbated by limited access to cardiology specialists and outdated equipment in public hospitals.

2.4.2 Wearable and Consumer-Grade Heart Monitoring Devices
Over the past decade, wearable health technologies have revolutionized the way individuals monitor and manage their heart health. Unlike traditional hospital-based systems that require clinical settings and trained personnel, wearable devices offer real-time, on-the-go monitoring of vital parameters such as heart rate, rhythm, and physical activity. This shift reflects the growing demand for patient-centric, preventive healthcare models and is particularly impactful in improving access to cardiac monitoring for populations with limited proximity to medical facilities (Bayoumy et al., 2021).
Consumer-grade wearables such as smartwatches (e.g., Apple Watch, Samsung Galaxy Watch, Fitbit) have integrated photoplethysmography (PPG) and single-lead ECG sensors capable of detecting irregular heart rhythms. These devices allow users to perform on-demand ECG recordings and receive alerts for abnormal rhythms like atrial fibrillation. The Apple Watch, for instance, has received FDA clearance for its ECG feature, highlighting the increasing clinical acceptance of consumer devices in mainstream cardiovascular medicine (Hughes et al., 2023).
In addition to wrist-based wearables, other form factors include chest-strap heart rate monitors, patch-based ECG recorders, and smart textiles. Chest-strap monitors, commonly used by athletes, offer higher accuracy during intense physical activity by minimizing motion artifacts. Patch-based systems such as the Zio Patch provide continuous multi-day ECG recording in a discreet, adhesive form, and are increasingly being used as alternatives to Holter monitors in outpatient settings. These innovations improve user comfort and adherence while supporting extended monitoring durations (A., 2025).
Despite their convenience and accessibility, consumer-grade wearables have several limitations. Most utilize single-lead ECGs, which provide a limited view of the heart’s electrical activity and may not detect more complex arrhythmias or regional abnormalities that require multi-lead systems. Additionally, data accuracy can vary based on factors such as skin tone, motion, sensor placement, and device calibration. Consumer devices are also susceptible to false positives, which can cause unnecessary anxiety or lead to overburdening of clinical systems with unverified alerts (Bayoumy et al., 2021).
Another concern is data privacy and security. As these devices often sync with cloud-based platforms and mobile applications, user health data may be vulnerable to unauthorized access if not properly encrypted or protected by regulations such as HIPAA or GDPR. Moreover, in low-resource settings, the high cost of premium wearables and limited smartphone penetration may still hinder widespread adoption.
Nevertheless, wearable cardiac monitors represent a significant step forward in democratizing heart health. When paired with mobile applications, AI-powered analytics, and telemedicine platforms, these devices can offer powerful tools for early detection, remote patient monitoring, and chronic disease management. Their ability to continuously gather health data empowers users to make informed lifestyle decisions while also enabling healthcare providers to intervene earlier in the disease process (Hughes et al., 2023).
2.5 Machine Learning in ECG Analysis
Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a critical technology in the field of biomedical signal processing, particularly for the analysis of electrocardiogram (ECG) data. The ECG signal, which represents the heart's electrical activity over time, is inherently complex, dynamic, and often influenced by noise and physiological variability. Traditional methods of ECG interpretation rely heavily on expert knowledge and manual analysis, which can be time-consuming, prone to human error, and impractical for large-scale or continuous monitoring. Machine learning offers a data-driven alternative, allowing for automated detection, classification, and prediction of cardiac events by identifying patterns within ECG waveforms that may be imperceptible to the human eye (Mincholé et al., 2019).
In typical ECG classification workflows, supervised learning algorithms are trained on labeled datasets where each ECG recording is annotated according to the type of rhythm or abnormality it contains. Popular algorithms include support vector machines (SVMs), k-nearest neighbors (k-NN), decision trees, random forests, and naïve Bayes classifiers. These models rely on carefully engineered features derived from the ECG signal—such as RR intervals, QRS complex width, heart rate variability, and morphological attributes of the P wave and T wave. By learning the statistical relationships between these features and the corresponding clinical labels, the model becomes capable of classifying new, unseen ECG signals into categories such as normal sinus rhythm, atrial fibrillation, or premature ventricular contractions. One of the main strengths of machine learning in this context is its adaptability; with sufficient training data and well-selected features, models can generalize to different populations and even handle moderately noisy inputs with acceptable accuracy (Luz et al., 2016; Kailan et al., 2024).
Preprocessing of the ECG signal is a crucial step in ensuring that machine learning algorithms perform reliably. Raw ECG data often contains noise from muscle contractions, motion artifacts, baseline wander due to respiration, and power line interference. Standard signal processing techniques such as bandpass filtering, notch filtering, and baseline correction are applied before feature extraction. For example, adaptive filtering techniques have proven effective in enhancing ECG signal quality by suppressing interference while preserving critical waveform features (Alla and Nayak, 2024). Once the signal is cleaned, time-domain and frequency-domain features are computed and structured into vectors suitable for model training. In cases where datasets suffer from class imbalance—such as having significantly fewer samples of rare arrhythmias—techniques like oversampling, data augmentation, and cost-sensitive learning are employed to improve the model’s sensitivity to underrepresented classes (Kailan et al., 2024).
The performance of machine learning models in ECG analysis is typically evaluated using metrics such as accuracy, sensitivity (recall), specificity, precision, F1-score, and the area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. These metrics provide insights into how well the model can distinguish between various cardiac conditions, and more importantly, how reliably it can detect life-threatening anomalies. High sensitivity is critical in clinical applications to avoid false negatives, which could result in missed diagnoses. Precision, on the other hand, helps minimize false alarms, which is particularly important in wearable or home-based monitoring systems where unnecessary alerts can cause anxiety or reduce user trust (Naidu et al., 2023).
Numerous studies have demonstrated that classical machine learning models, when combined with robust signal preprocessing and relevant feature engineering, can achieve strong performance in ECG classification tasks. For instance, support vector machines have been shown to effectively classify arrhythmias using waveform morphology and timing intervals (Li et al., 2022). In large public datasets such as the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database, traditional classifiers have reached classification accuracies of 85% to 95% for common rhythm disorders (Luz et al., 2016). Moreover, machine learning-based ECG analysis systems have the potential to be deployed on mobile and embedded devices, making them ideal for real-time monitoring in remote or underserved areas (Kailan et al., 2024).
Despite its promise, the application of machine learning in ECG interpretation is not without challenges. Models trained on one dataset may not generalize well to another due to differences in sampling rates, noise characteristics, or population demographics. Additionally, machine learning models can be sensitive to minor signal distortions, and their performance may degrade significantly in the presence of artifact-laden data. Ensuring robustness, interpretability, and clinical reliability remains an ongoing area of research. Nonetheless, the use of machine learning in ECG analysis continues to evolve, enabling faster, more accurate, and more scalable approaches to cardiac monitoring and diagnosis.

2.6 Deep Learning Approaches in ECG Analysis
Deep learning has become a cornerstone in modern ECG analysis, offering highly accurate and efficient solutions for automatic detection of cardiac abnormalities. Unlike traditional machine learning methods that rely on handcrafted features, deep learning models are capable of learning hierarchical patterns directly from raw ECG data. This end-to-end approach significantly improves detection accuracy, especially in cases involving subtle waveform variations or overlapping arrhythmias. 

2.6.1 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for ECG Feature Extraction
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a class of deep learning models inspired by the visual processing mechanisms of the human brain. Originally developed for image and pattern recognition, CNNs have proven to be highly effective for analyzing one-dimensional time-series data such as ECG signals. Their primary strength lies in their ability to automatically extract spatial features—such as wave amplitude, morphology, and slope—from the raw input without requiring manual feature engineering (Liu et al., 2021).
In ECG applications, CNNs use convolutional layers with learned filters that slide over the input signal to detect local patterns including the QRS complex, P wave, and T wave. These filters identify essential waveform characteristics like peak shapes, rising and falling edges, and segment durations, which are critical in distinguishing between normal and abnormal heart rhythms. Advanced CNN architectures have even been trained to isolate specific components such as P waves with high precision, aiding in the diagnosis of conditions like atrial enlargement and atrial fibrillation (Costandy et al., 2020).
The effectiveness of CNNs is enhanced by the use of activation functions (typically ReLU), pooling layers to reduce dimensionality, and dropout techniques to prevent overfitting. These layers work in tandem to extract increasingly abstract representations of ECG features, allowing the model to generalize well across patients with varying heart conditions and signal quality (Ansari et al., 2023). A significant advantage of CNNs in real-world ECG analysis is their resilience to signal noise and variability. In practice, ECG signals captured from wearable or portable devices often contain artifacts caused by body movement, respiration, and electrical interference. CNN-based models have shown strong performance in denoising such signals, especially when implemented through architectures like fully convolutional denoising autoencoders (Chiang et al., 2019). This noise tolerance makes CNNs particularly suitable for deployment in ambulatory and home-based cardiac monitoring systems.
CNNs have demonstrated high accuracy in arrhythmia classification tasks using datasets such as the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database, often achieving classification accuracies exceeding 90% in multi-class scenarios. Their computational efficiency and scalability make them a strong candidate for mobile health applications, as they can be optimized through quantization and model compression techniques for real-time inference on edge devices (Liu et al., 2021).

2.6.2 Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTMs) for Temporal Sequence Learning
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a specialized type of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) designed to learn and retain long-range dependencies in sequential data. Unlike standard RNNs, which are limited by vanishing or exploding gradient problems, LSTMs incorporate memory cells and gating mechanisms that allow them to manage the flow of information across time steps. This makes them especially well-suited for analyzing electrocardiogram (ECG) signals, where meaningful patterns often unfold over multiple heartbeats rather than within a single waveform.
In ECG analysis, LSTMs are particularly effective for capturing temporal relationships between successive cardiac cycles, such as variations in RR intervals, sequences of waveform shapes, and gradual rhythm changes. The LSTM’s internal architecture—comprising forget, input, and output gates—enables it to dynamically preserve or discard historical information depending on its relevance to the current context. This makes LSTMs highly capable of detecting arrhythmias that are intermittent or evolve slowly over time, such as paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, bradycardia, or episodes of ventricular tachycardia (Dutta et al., 2021).
LSTM models can process raw ECG segments or feature-based input sequences, such as time-domain HRV metrics, frequency-domain measures, or morphological descriptors. These sequences are typically divided into sliding windows, which the network interprets in temporal context. One of the strengths of LSTM networks is their adaptability to variable-length sequences, which allows them to function in both offline diagnostic tools and real-time applications where signal length may not be fixed (Malashin et al., 2024).
Compared to convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which are optimized for spatial pattern recognition in fixed-length input, LSTMs excel in learning the progression and recurrence of cardiac rhythms. For instance, while a CNN might effectively classify the morphology of a QRS complex, an LSTM can recognize the cyclical appearance of premature beats or missed intervals that signal more complex rhythm disorders. In recent studies, LSTM-based models have achieved impressive results in multi-class classification of arrhythmias using public datasets like the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database, especially when trained with context-aware input that spans several heartbeats (Dutta et al., 2021).
Moreover, LSTMs are increasingly being adopted in hybrid and ensemble architectures, including attention mechanisms and auto-encoder frameworks, to enhance their temporal reasoning capabilities. Models such as MED-NET have demonstrated that LSTM auto-encoders can successfully learn representations of normal ECG patterns and detect anomalies through reconstruction error, making them suitable not only for classification but also for unsupervised detection of unseen or rare cardiac events (Dutta et al., 2021). The robustness of LSTM models across varying signal qualities and patient conditions also makes them attractive for deployment in wearable, mobile, and edge-based ECG monitoring systems. With frameworks like TensorFlow Lite and PyTorch Mobile, lightweight LSTM models can be optimized for real-time inference even on low-power devices, enabling continuous cardiac monitoring outside hospital settings.

2.6.3 CNN-LSTM Hybrid Models for End-to-End ECG Analysis
While both Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in ECG analysis, each has distinct strengths—CNNs excel at spatial feature extraction, whereas LSTMs are ideal for capturing temporal dependencies. To leverage the advantages of both architectures, researchers have developed CNN-LSTM hybrid models that combine the two into a single, end-to-end trainable framework. This fusion enables more robust and accurate analysis of ECG signals by integrating morphological and sequential information in a unified deep learning pipeline (Reddy et al., 2025).
In a typical CNN-LSTM model for ECG classification, the input signal—either raw or preprocessed—is first passed through a series of convolutional layers to extract local features such as QRS morphology, P wave contour, and signal amplitude variation. These spatial features are then passed into one or more LSTM layers that model temporal dependencies between successive beats. This structure enables the model to identify not only localized waveform anomalies but also how those patterns evolve over time, which is crucial for diagnosing complex and intermittent arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation and ventricular ectopic beats (Petmezas et al., 2021).
The combination of CNNs and LSTMs also enhances the model's resilience to noisy or imbalanced datasets. CNN layers help suppress noise by emphasizing high-level morphological features, while the LSTM layers track sequential trends in heart activity. This makes hybrid models particularly useful for real-time ECG monitoring where signal artifacts, rhythm fluctuations, and class imbalance are common challenges (Sun et al., 2024). Several studies have demonstrated that CNN-LSTM architectures significantly outperform their standalone counterparts in both binary and multi-class arrhythmia classification tasks, with reported improvements in sensitivity, specificity, and overall accuracy (Zabihi et al., 2024).
An additional advantage of these models is their compatibility with attention mechanisms and squeeze-and-excitation (SE) blocks, which allow the network to assign dynamic weights to important time steps or feature maps. These enhancements have been shown to improve interpretability and diagnostic performance by guiding the model’s focus toward clinically significant segments of the ECG trace (Sun et al., 2024). CNN-LSTM architectures are also well-suited for deployment on edge devices in mobile and IoT-based cardiac monitoring systems. Through model compression techniques such as quantization and pruning, the hybrid networks can be optimized for low-latency inference on resource-constrained hardware, allowing for continuous monitoring in remote or ambulatory settings without reliance on cloud infrastructure (Reddy et al., 2025).
Despite their strengths, CNN-LSTM models can be computationally intensive during training due to the large number of parameters and layered architecture. To address this, researchers have adopted strategies such as transfer learning, data augmentation, and multi-scale feature encoding to enhance performance while maintaining efficiency. Moreover, hybrid approaches that combine machine learning classifiers with deep learning-based feature extraction are being explored to further improve adaptability and reduce training time (Zabihi et al., 2024).

2.7 Mobile Application for Real-Time ECG Monitoring
The use of mobile applications in cardiovascular health monitoring has gained significant momentum in recent years, fueled by advancements in mobile computing, wearable sensors, and artificial intelligence (AI). As healthcare shifts toward patient-centric, continuous monitoring models, mobile apps have become essential tools that not only visualize and store health data but also play an active role in real-time diagnostics and preventive care. These apps function as the interface between end-users and physiological data streams, offering accessibility, portability, and personalized health insights at scale (Oyeniyi, 2024).
Modern mobile app development has been propelled by cross-platform frameworks such as React Native, Flutter, and Kotlin, which allow developers to create highly responsive applications compatible with both Android and iOS ecosystems using a shared codebase. React Native supports seamless integration with third-party modules for health data capture, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) communication, cloud storage, and push notifications—critical for applications in digital health. Frameworks like this are particularly useful in mHealth systems, where modularity and device compatibility are crucial for reliability and user experience (Tukiainen, 2021). This flexibility is vital for real-time ECG monitoring, where the mobile app must interface continuously with wearable sensors and deliver both visual and auditory feedback to the user in case of cardiac anomalies.
Parallel to mobile development, embedded AI frameworks such as TensorFlow Lite, ONNX Runtime, and Core ML have made it possible to integrate lightweight yet powerful machine learning models directly within mobile applications. These embedded models are capable of performing on-device inference, meaning they can analyze incoming ECG data in real-time without requiring internet connectivity. This architecture improves performance by reducing latency and enhances privacy by ensuring sensitive health data remains local to the user’s device (Dini et al., 2024). In cardiovascular applications, AI models embedded in mobile apps can classify heart rhythms, detect arrhythmias, and alert users or caregivers before symptoms escalate into emergencies.
Research and industry alike have validated the impact of such systems. For instance, applications like KardiaMobile by AliveCor, Apple Health, and Qardio have demonstrated that mobile apps—when integrated with wearable ECG sensors and AI algorithms—can provide clinical-grade heart monitoring outside traditional healthcare environments. These solutions have been shown to detect atrial fibrillation, bradycardia, and tachycardia with high sensitivity, making them valuable tools in early diagnosis and chronic disease management (Chauhan et al., 2025). According to Chauhan and colleagues, these systems are not only beneficial for at-risk patients but also promote active health engagement, enabling users to self-monitor and manage their heart health more confidently and proactively.
Moreover, the integration of AI with mobile health apps enhances personalized care delivery, which is especially important in low-resource or rural settings where access to cardiologists and ECG machines is limited. AI-based mobile solutions can act as digital triage tools—prioritizing patients, flagging critical cases, and reducing the burden on healthcare facilities. As highlighted by Oyeniyi (2024), this aligns with the broader movement toward equitable and scalable healthcare systems, where technology reduces the dependence on geography and infrastructure.

2.8 Review of Related Works
Recent advancements in ECG-based cardiac arrhythmia detection have centered around deep learning and hybrid machine learning systems to improve classification accuracy and generalizability across ECG datasets. One standout study by Reddy et al. (2025) introduced a Multi-Scale Convolutional LSTM-Dense Network (MS-CLDNet), which successfully addressed issues such as signal noise and class imbalance. By combining DenseNet for feature refinement, CNNs for multi-resolution pattern extraction, and BiLSTM for capturing temporal dependencies, the model reached an impressive 98.22% classification accuracy on the MIT-BIH arrhythmia dataset.
Similarly, Sahoo et al. (2020) conducted an extensive survey of machine learning techniques used in ECG-based arrhythmia detection. Their review emphasized the pipeline of ECG analysis, including signal preprocessing (e.g., noise filtering and baseline correction), QRS complex detection, and the use of feature extraction techniques like time-domain statistics and wavelet transforms. Complementing this, Moskalenko et al. (2020) proposed a UNet-inspired neural network architecture for ECG waveform segmentation. Their model, designed to detect the onsets and offsets of P, QRS, and T waves, demonstrated exceptional performance with F1-scores of 97.8% for P-waves, 99.5% for T-waves, and 99.9% for QRS complexes, all while maintaining a lightweight computational profile suitable for embedded systems.
Further exploring wearable ECG solutions, Ozkan et al. (2024) presented a Tele-ECG monitoring system that integrates textile electrodes, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), smartphone interfacing, and cloud-based analytics for long-term, real-time cardiac monitoring. The system offers emergency alerts and location tracking, with a 14-day battery life and 99.23% correlation with traditional Holter monitors. Similarly, Petmezas et al. (2021) developed a hybrid CNN-LSTM model that combines spatial feature extraction with temporal modeling to detect atrial fibrillation. The model, trained on the MIT-BIH AFib dataset, achieved high sensitivity (97.87%) and specificity (99.29%), using focal loss to effectively address data imbalance.
In benchmarking efforts, Strodthoff et al. (2021) evaluated a range of deep learning architectures on the PTB-XL dataset, a large publicly available 12-lead ECG corpus. They found that ResNet- and Inception-based CNNs performed best in multi-label classification tasks, including ECG statement prediction and demographic inference. The study emphasized the utility of transfer learning and contributed insights on model interpretability and uncertainty estimation, paving the way for more clinically robust ECG diagnostic tools. In parallel, Gao et al. (2019) engineered a wearable heart monitor utilizing flexible capacitive electrodes and a streamlined analog front-end with only four op-amps. Their system demonstrated signal quality on par with commercial sensors—even during motion—while eliminating the downsides of gel-based electrodes.
For mobile and low-resource applications, Skoglösa (2021) designed a budget-friendly mobile ECG diagnostic system based on Arduino, Bluetooth transmission, and an Android display app. He evaluated Pan-Tompkins and moving average filters for QRS detection, noting that while the moving average was faster, Pan-Tompkins provided better accuracy and stability. Though real-time visualization was achieved, performance was occasionally hindered by power-line interference and limited computational robustness. 
Hammad et al. (2018) focused on ECG signal classification using 13 extracted features from the MIT-BIH dataset. They tested multiple classifiers including neural networks, SVMs, and k-NN, ultimately proposing a custom logic-based classifier that mimicked expert diagnosis and achieved 99% accuracy—outperforming conventional methods. Supporting these findings, Xiao et al. (2023) conducted a systematic review of 368 studies and concluded that CNNs remain dominant in ECG classification, with hybrid approaches rapidly gaining traction. They also highlighted persistent challenges in inter-patient generalization and stressed the need for richer datasets, adaptive preprocessing, and novel architectures to ensure practical deployment.
Ansari et al. (2023) presented a chronological review of deep learning models for arrhythmia detection from 2017 to 2023, highlighting the superior performance of CNNs, RNNs, and hybrid models such as CNN-LSTM architectures. They emphasized the need for comparative benchmarking and intertextual analysis to guide new researchers and proposed development pipeline standards to support future scalability. Their findings also pointed out that an accuracy threshold of 96% or higher is generally required for clinical viability.
In practical application, Yamamoto et al. (2020) introduced a contactless ECG reconstruction system using Doppler sensors combined with a hybrid CNN-LSTM model. The system achieved a 0.86 correlation between predicted and actual ECG signals, demonstrating that spatial-temporal analysis could serve as an effective alternative to conventional electrode-based measurements in select environments. Similarly, Georgieva-Tsaneva et al. (2024) designed a portable ECG system incorporating HRV metrics, fall detection, and embedded sensors to issue emergency alerts in syncopal events. By leveraging linear and nonlinear HRV analysis tools—such as SDNN, Poincaré plots, and phase space diagrams—the system proved especially useful in elderly and rural care contexts.
In the educational domain, Lima et al. (2019) developed a mobile application, ECG Fácil, aimed at improving ECG interpretation skills among medical students. The app received high usability ratings (System Usability Scale score of 85.3) and positive user feedback, although long-term retention and real-world diagnostic impact remain untested. Meanwhile, Rahman et al. (2019) built an IoT-based monitoring system using ECG and temperature sensors with Raspberry Pi as the processing hub. Real-time features included live ECG graphing, SMS alerts, and video calling, enhancing the capacity for home-based monitoring of high-risk patients.
On the diagnostic frontier, Gustafsson et al. (2022) trained a ResNet-based deep learning model on nearly half a million ECGs for detecting myocardial infarction subtypes (STEMI, NSTEMI). The model achieved a C-statistic of 0.991 for STEMI classification—outperforming emergency physicians and commercial diagnostic platforms—underscoring its potential in critical care triage. Likewise, Al-Zaiti et al. (2023) introduced a Random Forest model for diagnosing occlusion myocardial infarction (OMI) using ECG data from 7,313 patients. Their system outperformed FDA-approved tools and cardiologist interpretation, achieving an AUROC of 0.91 and producing a novel OMI risk score that enhances real-time clinical decision-making. In related work, Aziz et al. (2021) developed a unique ECG classification approach using TERMA (Time Evolving Recurrent Motion Analysis) and the Fractional Fourier Transform to enhance peak detection. The model, coupled with MLP and SVM classifiers, was tested on the large-scale SPH dataset and achieved excellent cross-database generalization, with low computational complexity suitable for scalable clinical deployment.
Alimbayeva et al. (2024) presented a wearable ECG monitoring system based on a single-lead device using the ADS1298 chip and STM32L151xD microcontroller. The system features multi-stage noise filtering, unsupervised signal clustering (via K-means), and automated detection of PQRST complexes. Machine learning models were used to detect cardiac anomalies, with CNNs achieving the best performance (92.6% accuracy). Complementing this work, Chen et al. (2019) proposed a smart ECG system with a two-stage predictive model. The first layer detects severe abnormalities (red alerts), while the second tracks subtle patient-specific deviations (yellow alerts), increasing the likelihood of early warnings by up to 10%, thereby enabling timely intervention ahead of acute cardiac events.

2.9 Research Gap
Despite the impressive strides in ECG analysis using machine learning and deep learning, most existing systems either require high computational resources, lack real-time mobile integration, or do not adequately support low-resource and rural healthcare environments. Many reviewed studies focus on controlled datasets, overlook deployment on lightweight platforms, or lack end-to-end solutions combining wearable hardware, mobile applications, and intelligent arrhythmia detection. Additionally, challenges such as inter-patient variability, limited offline functionality, and absence of culturally adaptable alert systems persist. This project addresses these gaps by developing an IoT-based heart monitoring and heart-attack detection system that integrates CNN-LSTM architecture with a real-time mobile application, enabling offline analysis, emergency alerting, and usability in resource-constrained environments.

3.2.2 Prototyping
The prototyping phase involved assembling the heart monitoring system on a solderless breadboard to enable quick and flexible circuit connections. This approach allowed for easy modifications and troubleshooting during initial hardware testing. The breadboard served as a temporary platform for integrating key components, including the AD8232 ECG sensor, NodeMCU ESP8266, OLED display, TP4056 charging module, and the boost converter. Jumper wires were used extensively to establish non-permanent connections, ensuring adaptability during frequent revisions.
Each subsystem was tested individually before full integration. The ECG module was first verified for stable signal acquisition using serial output, the microcontroller for Wi-Fi connectivity and ADC functionality, and the OLED display for real-time waveform rendering. The power system was also validated to ensure stable voltage regulation. This setup helped refine the wiring layout and confirm the correctness of electrical connections, serving as a precursor to a more compact and durable implementation.

3.2.3 Assembling
The assembling stage involved integrating all the electronic components into a cohesive and portable unit suitable for continuous use. The process required careful alignment of the sensor, microcontroller, display, and power units to ensure compactness and usability. Each component was securely fixed to a small, non-conductive mounting base using double-sided adhesive and mounting tape to avoid movement during operation.
The AD8232 ECG sensor was placed in a position that allowed the 3-lead electrodes to be easily routed to the user’s chest area, ensuring good contact and minimal noise. The NodeMCU was mounted beside it with spacers to reduce physical strain on the jumper wires and prevent accidental disconnections. The OLED display was positioned in a way that enabled easy visibility for real-time waveform monitoring and system status updates.
Power connections from the 3.7 V lithium-ion battery passed through the TP4056 charging module and then to a boost converter, which stepped the voltage up to 5 V for the NodeMCU and OLED display. All modules were wired using jumper wires, and connections were reinforced with electrical tape to prevent loose contacts. Excess wiring was managed using zip ties to maintain a tidy layout and minimize the risk of interference or short circuits. Once the assembly was complete, the system was tested as a whole to confirm that the ECG signal could be acquired, transmitted, and visualized correctly, and that the mobile application received and processed data as expected. This finalized prototype served as the complete functional version, ready for field evaluation and user feedback.
